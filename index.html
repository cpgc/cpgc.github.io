Hello, comrade! We are the Communist Party of Glendowie College, a fun, radical far-left-wing revolutionary party with ambitions ranging from the revolutionary overthrow of Mr Dykes to the revolutionary overthrow of the capitalist system.

Communism is a very strong, powerful political movement in many countries. There are approximately 232 million communists in the world, and more and more people are joining the communist movement as they become disillusioned with the capitalist order. There are many militant communist parties around the world, including the YPG in Rojava (one of the main forces fighting against ISIS at the moment) and the communist guerilla groups of India, although, to any government officials reading this, we are not yet ready to take up arms against the New Zealand government. Emphasis on the word “yet”.

Rojava isn’t often talked about in the media, because it is a tax-less country based on principles of direct democracy, gender equality, and sustainability which has abolished capitalism.

Communism has been very successful. Half of the world’s countries used to be Red. For example, when the Bolshevik Revolution overthrew the Russian government, Russia was one of the most poverty-ridden, unstable, famine-stricken, backwards countries on the Earth.

With the help of communism, Russia (later becoming the USSR, which grew to include 33 countries) doubled life expectancy, increased the size of its economy by a factor of 300, and became a global economic, political, and military superpower in just under thirty years, and it did it all while guaranteeing

  • every citizen had a job and a home (no unemployment or homelessness)
  • old people received generous pensions after retirement
  • complete economic equality of the sexes
  • free healthcare and high-quality education
  • paid maternity leave
  • limits on working hours
  • complete economic stability (i.e no economic crashes)
  • very inexpensive, high-quality public transportation
  • crime rates close to zero

The USSR was called a “second world” country.

In the Chinese Communist Revolution for 1949, the People’s Republic of China, lead by the ideas of Mao Tse-Tung, was born. Carefully choreographed economic planning, a feature of all communist countries, allowed China to flourish into the economic superpower it is today.

In fact, everywhere people have risen up against capitalism to create a fairer, communist society, the success story has been the same. However, it seems the more successful communism is, the greater the effort capitalists expend in ensuring that workers stay obedient to capitalism. Billions of dollars have been spent on disinformation and propaganda against communism, and millions of people have been jailed or executed for being communists. The first people to be sent to Nazi concentration camps were communists, and the first political party to be banned in the United States was the Communist Party.

To sort out this confusion, we will clarify what communism is and isn’t.

What is communism?

Communism, in short, is the movement for the liberation of all people from capitalism. It achieves this through the following three things:

  1. The abolition of private property.
  2. The abolition of exploitation.
  3. The planning of the economy for the common interests of all people.

 

Abolition of private property

Contrary to what many people think, private property is not a “thing” or a physical object – it is the relationship between a person and a thing. Think about it like this: a car just by itself is not property, because nobody owns it. If a person says “I now own this car!”, that car then becomes the property of that person, because property is the relationship between the car and the person.

Property exists only because of violence. If you tried to steal Mr Dykes’ car, then Mr Dykes would call the police (an appeal to authority) and the police would remove the car from your possession (violence). If you tried to take someone’s ballpoint pen, then they would either tell the teacher (an appeal to authority) or physically try to wrench it out of your hands (violence). In societies without an authority to appeal to, like in the wild west, property must be backed up with violence. If you want to say “this is mine, and only I can use it” then you need a gun.

So what’s bad about private property?

Well, to answer that, think about houses. 65% of people in New Zealand don’t own the homes they live in. They pay money to a landlord – if they don’t, the landlord can forcefully evict them (violence). Why do they have to pay money? Because the landlord “owns” the house – i.e, he has a piece of paper that the government recognises that says “I own that house, I get to do with it what I want”. In New Zealand, there are plenty of empty, vacant houses that no one uses – simply because the person who bought the house said to themselves “I own that house, I’m just going to let it sit there and increase in value for a few decades”.

In the United States, the problem is even worse – there are areas of towns and cities filled with tens of thousands of houses that nobody lives in, while those same areas are filled with people who don’t have homes. The corporations which own those houses have said to themselves “well, I’m not going to let people live in those houses for free because there’s nothing in it for me!” So they just let the houses waste away without maintenance – all 18.4 million of them.

Think about this scenario too: hundreds of people have just been fired from their jobs. Their families are hungry and penniless, so they take apples from a supermarket without paying for them. The supermarket calls in the police, and the families are sent to prison for violating private property. Later, the supermarket finds that since no one had any money, it couldn’t sell any of the apples, and throws them into the bin.

Clearly both situations can be fixed very easily: abolish private property. People stop having to pay most of their income to their landlord on the threat of being evicted, homelessness disappears, vacant houses are filled, and people get apples to eat. Hundreds, thousands of problems are fixed with just one solution. Communists have a famous phrase: “from each according to his ability, to each according to his needs!”

 

Abolition of exploitation

Most of us have a pretty good idea of what “exploitation” is. Being treated unfairly by someone so that they can benefit from our work. Like someone tricking you into painting their whole house for them and giving just a dollar in return. Or building a chair. Or baking bread. Or taking orders at a restaurant. Or…

Hold on a minute…

Isn’t that essentially every job? Doing something for someone and getting short-changed on it? If you baked a cake for someone and they said thank you very much, gave you two dollars, and proceeded to sell the cake for fifteen dollars – it’s easy to imagine how pissed off you would be. Very pissed off.

After all, if he sold the cake for two dollars, what’s in it for him? But here’s the thing: if there are other people also selling cakes, he can’t sell the cake for fifty dollars because his competitors are selling identical cakes for less money, and he’ll go out of business because no one will buy his ridiculously expensive cakes. So he clearly can’t profit from selling it “over”. But what can he do? He can give you less money for making the cake. He could give you one dollar for making the cake instead of two dollars. Profits don’t come from selling over, they come from paying under.

The Australian communist movement has a good, snappy phrase for this: “all profits are unpaid wages.”

 

We call the cake-bosses the capitalists, and the cake-makers the workers. The distinction is between people who live off the labour of other people versus people who labour to live. And this ties into private property too! If you had a house, food, and water, what need would there be to work? Because the factories where the cakes are made are under the private ownership of the capitalists, you must pay money in order to get access to those cakes. What’s the only way to get money as a worker under capitalism? Working for a capitalist. And you have to work for a capitalist, even when you’re exploited, because there’s no other alternative.

Could you imagine how richer you’d be if you weren’t constantly exploited in every job you went into? If we abolished private property, there wouldn’t be people who could say “I own this and you don’t, so you have to work for me in order to stay alive” – there wouldn’t be a capitalist class. The decisions which capitalists make in capitalism, such as what gets produced, how much of it gets produced, what color it gets produced in, working environments etc., would be decided democratically by workers.
